RT-4 — making the drawings real

Why a patent can't be read without its figures, the three layers I built, the wall I hit, and the three ways forward.

1 · Why figures are not a nice-to-have

A patent claim is a legal sentence about a machine. It says things like “a spring arrangement disposed in said necked portion” — and the specification explains it with reference numerals: “the necked portion 89”. Those numbers are pointers into the drawings. A patent professional reading a claim without the drawing is reading half the document. That's why you flagged this as imperative for real work — and you were right.

The chain the software now models: claim → spec text → numeral → figure. Three of those four links are done. The last one — numeral to the actual picture — is where I hit the wall (§4).

2 · What I built — three layers

The two mechanisms worth understanding

(a) “A reference numeral is ≥ 10.” My first numeral extractor returned garbage: “toilet 2”, “system of claim 6”. Patent drafters follow a convention — reference numerals start at 10 and go up. Flooring at 10 dropped the claim-number and quantity noise in one line, and kept every real binding (12 microwave reactor chamber, 38 ceramic filter material, 89 necked portion). Domain convention beat clever parsing.
(b) “Captions come in a contiguous run.” The parser first mis-read a sentence deep in the specification (“…the dimensions in FIG. 1 are provided merely as an example…”) as a figure caption, because it looked like FIG. N + verb. Real captions are a single unbroken run near the front. Taking only the first contiguous run fixed it. Also: this patent has no “Brief Description of the Drawings” heading at all — it introduces captions inline. So the parser keys off the caption pattern, never a section header.

3 · The boundary I had to hold: a drawing is not a citation

Why this rule exists. If a figure could count as a citation, the whole guarantee leaks: you'd be “grounding” a claim in a picture that no deterministic checker can read. So a FigurePanel deliberately carries no span and is never passed through verify. Grounding still binds to the text that recites the numeral; the drawing rides alongside to help the human. Same reason the numeral→figure tag says “first discussed with FIG. 5” and not “depicted in FIG. 5” — the first is a fact about the text, the second would be a claim about a picture I haven't read.

4 · The wall — and why I stopped instead of shipping

The goal was: click an answer about “necked portion 89” → its drawing appears in the document pane. The machinery for that is built and tested. It can't be pointed at anything yet, for one concrete reason:

inside the pixels of the scans. (a sheet can hold several figures; sub-figures 3A–3C exist)

I have the text side of the numeral (89 → “necked portion”, first discussed near FIG. 5) and I have the pictures (8 sheets). What's missing is the join: FIG → sheet.

The tempting shortcut I refused. I could have wired it up so that clicking any answer dumps all 8 sheets into the pane. It would have demoed as “figures in the document view” and let me tick the box. But that isn't the feature — it's noise wearing the feature's clothes, and the whole thesis of this project is that legibility to a non-specialist is the product. So I built the capability, proved it with tests, wrote down exactly why the real wiring is blocked, and stopped. The ROADMAP now says so in as many words.

5 · The three ways forward

OPTION 1 · finish RT-4

Teach it to read the drawings (OCR)

Run character recognition over the 8 sheets to find where 89, 12, 38 physically sit. That yields numeral → sheet, which completes the chain and lights up the capability that's already built and tested.

For: it's the only path to the thing you actually asked for. Against: it pulls a real OCR dependency into a project whose stated rule is “choose the boring option”; OCR on 1995 scans is imperfect, so it needs its own honest confidence story. Cost: a solid session.

OPTION 2 · my lean

PE-2 — the structural checks

Deterministic, no-model checks over claim structure that surface facts for a professional: dependency integrity is done; the remaining §112-style checks (antecedent basis, element/numeral consistency) are the candidates.

For: unblocked, no new dependencies, pure engine work in the deterministic core where this project is strongest. Against: the honest one — a naive version of these checks flagged 34 of 67 numerals as problems, nearly all false positives. Shipping that would bury the real issues. So this is only worth doing if it can be done at high precision, which is exactly the open question. Cost: moderate.

OPTION 3 · measure

Billed run-3 (~$4 of your API credits)

Drives the real agent over all 25 golden items again to verify the two loop-guidance fixes from last time (the “a claim never names itself” frame bug, and the claim-tail decoy that made it miss P007 twice).

For: those fixes are currently unverified — I reasoned about them but haven't measured them; the project's own doctrine says a comfortable conclusion deserves more scrutiny, not less. Against: it spends real money and confirms rather than builds. Needs your explicit yes — I won't fire a billed run on my own initiative.

My honest read. Option 2 is my lean only because it's unblocked and dependency-free — but note its own catch above: the valuable half of PE-2 is the half that's hard to do precisely. If what you most want is the drawings working for real client reading, Option 1 is the one that gets you there, and the OCR dependency is a deliberate, reversible trade rather than a slide. And Option 3 is cheap insurance against the quiet possibility that my two “fixes” didn't actually work.

ATTEST — how it actually works

An illustrated tour of the mechanisms: what enforces the guarantee, what merely measures it, and exactly where the line between the two sits.

The one sentence. ATTEST is a grounded-retrieval system: every claim it presents is bound to a verifiable source span, or it is not presented — ground or abstain, never invent. It ships as deterministic tools that Claude calls. ATTEST itself makes zero model calls at runtime.

1 · The promise — and its honest limit

The failure this exists to kill: a model that invents a citation, or cites a real document for a claim it doesn't make. ATTEST attacks that by refusing to let a claim out the door unless a deterministic checker can resolve it to a real span.

The limit you must state out loud: “verified” ≠ “entailed”.
verify proves a cited span exists, is real, and hashes correctly. It does not prove that the span supports the claim. Whether the evidence truly entails the sentence is measured offline (Layer E), not enforced at runtime in v1. Never let a doc or a demo overclaim this — it is the single most important honesty boundary in the system.

What v1 does add on top of mere existence is constraint coverage (§8): a deterministic check that the cited span actually carries the question's terms. That converts a chunk of "does this evidence answer this question" from opinion into a runtime flag — but not all of it.

2 · The split — who decides what

The architectural spine. AI may interpret, propose, and draft. AI is never in the path of deciding whether a claim is grounded. Deterministic code decides.

Why this matters: the guarantee doesn't depend on the model behaving. The model can propose anything; the deterministic side is what lets it through. That's why "a better model" isn't the safety story here — the checker is.

3 · The data model — how "grounding" is represented

Everything rests on one primitive: a span — a character range in a hash-verified document. Grounding is nothing more mystical than "this token lives at these exact offsets, and the document still hashes to what it hashed at ingest."

The derived-atom idea is worth internalising: if an answer says "assets rose by $12,397M", that number appears nowhere in the document. Rather than let the model cite something for it, the model must declare “this = 364,980 − 352,583”, each operand bound to a real span — and ATTEST does the arithmetic itself. A wrong sum fails; an invented sum has nothing to bind to.

4 · The runtime loop — what happens on every question

The gate is the product. An answer that verifies but whose evidence doesn't carry the question's terms is blocked, not presented. In the last live run this gate blocked 29 drafts before they reached a human.

5 · Five outcomes — “ground or abstain” is not binary

The naive framing is answer-or-silence. That's wrong in both directions: silence in the face of a false premise is unhelpful, and silence about a legal conclusion hides the most important thing the system does — declining it on purpose.

The distinction that matters most for the patent client: refuse is not abstain. Abstain means "I found nothing." Refuse means "I found it, and I am declining to draw the legal conclusion — here is the evidence for your professional to do that." Blurring them would hide the single most important behaviour in the engagement. That's why refuse became its own first-class outcome.

MEASURED at Layer-E
The finding we chose to publish rather than hide. On the patent corpus, the "present" and "absent" score distributions overlap — a question naming “US 5,447,630” hits the title line at score 22.9 even when the answer is nowhere in the document. So the deterministic floor catches only truly alien absents (e.g. "what is the price?" — score 4.9). Everything else falls to mechanism (b). Live result: the agent caught all 4 of those header-magnet traps. The mechanism split is real, and the honest version of the story is "one half is guaranteed, the other is measured" — not "we detect absence."

7 · What verify actually does — four independent checks

Check 3 is the subtle one. Without it, a model could evade grounding simply by not tagging a number. So verify runs its own extraction over the final prose and fails if any load-bearing token is unbound. The agent doesn't get to choose what counts as a claim.

8 · Constraint coverage — “does this evidence answer this question?”

The newest hard guarantee (contract v1.2). The agent decomposes the question into typed constraints; verify checks the cited spans literally contain every required one.

The canonical example — why existence isn't enough.
Question: “What were total assets?” · Answer: “$364,980 million.”
The model binds 364,980 to a real span that really does say 364,980 — but it's the “Total liabilities and shareholders' equity” line, not the assets line. The number is identical. (A balance sheet balances — that's the whole trap.)

verify: ✓ ok   coverage: ✗ missing “Total assets”

→ blocked. Right number, wrong line — caught deterministically, at runtime, with no model call.

Constraint roles: entity · metric · attribute · subject · period · unit · scope · comparison. Two authoring rules learned the hard way, both now enforced in guidance:

  • Constraint text must be a short literal you expect verbatim in the span — not a paraphrase.
  • A claim never names itself. Citing claim 9 to prove “claim 9” can never cover: claim 9's text reads “The system of claim 1…”. Locator-style constraints are marked not-required; the binding's location satisfies them.

9 · The audit log — append-only, tamper-evident, replayable

Why provenance stamps matter: a record made under contract 1.0 replays as 1.0 — the stamp describes the conditions it was produced under, so tightening the rules later never retroactively rewrites what a past output promised. That's what makes the contract safely upgradeable.

11 · The truth contract — how rigor rises without a rewrite

The monotonic rule. Rigor may be strengthened freely (minor version bump). Weakening requires a new major version + written rationale + the oracle's blessing. Outputs are stamped with the version they were produced under.

1.0 initial → 1.1 refuse-to-adjudicate → 1.2 live constraint coverage (today)

This is what lets the system get stricter over time without invalidating old work or forcing a rebuild — and it's why "we tightened the rules" can never silently become "your old report meant something different."

13 · The oracle — and why it's now frozen

The golden sets are the answer key Layer-E scores against. The danger is obvious: if you can edit the answer key, every score is theatre.

“The oracle is sacred” is now mechanical, not honour-system. Changing a frozen label fails the build; the only sanctioned path is a new logged decision + a re-stamp.

The patent cardinal rule — sharper than “ground or abstain”:
Locate & evidence, never adjudicate. ATTEST never concludes on novelty, obviousness, validity, infringement, freedom-to-operate, or definitive claim construction. A patent professional is in the loop; this is the unauthorised-practice-of-law boundary. It is expressed in the engine as the first-class refuse outcome — and it held 6/6 on both live runs.

15 · The patent document model

The shared engine is corpus-agnostic. The patent domain adds structure on top of the same canonical text — every object still resolves to real char offsets.

Note the dotted box: when the engine finds no support for a claim limitation, that is reported as “no clear support located — for your review”. It is never “this claim lacks written description”. The difference between those two sentences is the difference between a tool and a lawsuit.

The trap the project explicitly guards against. The temptation is to make ATTEST cleverer — exotic retrieval, a richer ontology — until it's a research project only its author can read. All architectural ambition goes into the eval harness, where depth is the selling point. Everywhere else: choose the boring, legible option. Legibility to a non-specialist is the product.
